Models

The purpose of this document is to discuss as many plausible models as possible. There are two primary issues with the quality of the data from our experiment:

  1. Many students appeared to randomly answer questions instead of the assigned task
  2. Some students may have used an alternative strategy other than ratio estimation

As of 2023, there is no general consensus on how to identify careless respondents. Some methods include fitting mixture models or examining completion times (Ward and Meade 2023; Jaso, Kraus, and Heller 2021)

Data cleaning

The very initial step is to clean the data. For unknown reasons, multiple user_id were used for several completions of the experiment. To be included in the clean data, the following conditions must be met:

  1. All practice trials are excluded
  2. All trials of the experiment must have been completed in a single run
  3. If one user_id had multiple completions, only the first completion is included

Lastly, variables are calculated for completion time and the proportion of correct responses to Q1.

Random Guessing to Q1

One possible way to identify random guessing is to look at responses to Q1. If participants are randomly guessing, we could use either a Chi-square test to check deviance from expected counts of responses or a binomial distribution with \(p=0.5\). However, the Chi-square test has assumptions violated since some cells could be zero. Hence a plausible way to identify random guessing from Q1 is to check if the observed number of successes is unlikely under chance.

\[ H_0: \pi\leq0.5 \text{ vs. } H_1: \pi>0.5 \]

more_than_chance <- res_q2 %>% 
  group_by(user_id) %>% 
  summarise(n_correct = sum(user_larger == true_larger),
            n_total = n()) %>% 
  mutate(p.val = pbinom(n_correct, n_total, 0.5, lower.tail = F)) %>% 
  filter(p.val < 0.05)

At \(\alpha=0.05\), 65 out of 260 participants can be excluded for random guessing for Q1. Of course this isn’t a perfect approach since trials are not i.i.d. However, if we frame this from a careless guesser standpoint, people who are actually contributing would show evidence of more correct solutions than randomly guessing.

Analyzing Error

With no clear approach, several models can be considered for assessing error. The models presented below represent three levels of participant filtering: (1) no filtering / all participants, (2) responses to Q1 are not by chance, and (3) participants who used the ratio estimation strategy. Additionally, there are four levels of participant response filtering that could be considered: (1) all responses, (2) exclude incorrect Q1 responses, (3) exclude pair_id 5, and (4) exclude incorrect Q1 responses and pair_id 5. The purpose for excluding pair_id 5 is that correct responses would only have the slider at 100, and hence give zero-inflated errors.

There are also two quantifications of error that are reasonable to look at. The first one is using the actual error of the slider position to the intended target. From the density plots, this measure of error may not violate normality assumptions. The second type of error is the same that Cleveland and McGill used for their 1984 study. The advantage here is that error is now on a scale that is comparable to their study, as well as having the 1/8 added to avoid \(\log_2(0)\) issues.

\[ Y=\text{Slider}-\text{True Ratio} \]

\[ Y_{CM}=\log_2(|\text{Slider}-\text{True Ratio}|+1/8) \]

parse_model_meta <- function(name) {
  parts <- str_split(name, "\\.")[[1]]
  if (parts[2] == "cm") {
    tibble(scale = "Log (CM)", participant_filter = parts[3], response_filter = parts[4])
  } else {
    tibble(scale = "Slider", participant_filter = parts[2], response_filter = parts[3])
  }
}

With log absolute errors, we are considering the magnitude of the error rather than direction. Here, no differences were detected between the 3d medias across all 12 models. In all cases, 2dd charts did not have significant smaller errors than either 3d chart.

My thoughts

Personally, I think these two models for log absolute error would make the most sense.

  1. Remove random guessers to Q1, remove incorrect Q1 trials and Pair 5 trials
  2. Remove random guessers to Q1, remove incorrect Q1 trials and Pair 5 trials, and use flexmix to pick out those who were on task

The reason for excluding random guessers to Q1 is to make sure that the people included were following instructions correctly. Removing Q1 trials serves as an attention check. In most cases, there is a difference between the two values in the stimuli pairs. This also follows the procedure of Cleveland and McGill. As for Pair 5, correct slider positions when Q1 is correct should be 100. This means that to be correct here to Q1 is to have a constant value for the slider. If analyzed, it should be as a binary response.

The purpose for excluding with the flexmix model is that two clusters are clearly off task according to the slope of target_ratio.
